For posterity, what follows is a recounting of the discovery and translation of the tablet now known as the Diluculum Versum.

A research expedition helmed by Knight Maven Kiona Duemois ventured south to the Duchy of Nokistra. Among her retinue were myself, Jean Luminescu, and a young squire named Haskell Stralucci.

While Nokistra was under Alloyed Consortium rule, all industry was strictly controlled. Following Duke Gorkole's rebellion, Nokistra and Historia signed a bilateral treaty. Nokistra was recognized as an official Duchy of Historia with the freedom to largely self-govern and Historia was granted some special provisions. One such provision was free and open access to any archaeological discoveries made by Nokistra. With the reestablishment of Nokistran controlled mining operations a wealth of artifacts has been uncovered, and just such an item was the reason for Kiona's expedition.

The trip to Nokisgrad was uneventful. Upon arrival we were given rooms in the ducal palace which were exceedingly comfortable and spacious. I still occasionally awaken, only to feel immense disappointment at finding that I am not in those palacial quarters, but my small apartment.

We wasted no time and began our work as soon as we arrived, not resting until first light. The tablet was made of a strange metal that neither Haskell nor myself could identify, though Lady Kiona called it Leymetal. Though I had never seen any in person I was familiar with the element by name and was baffled that it had been used for a mere tablet. Leymetal is an incredibly valuable resource used in artifice and during the course of our investigations we determined the tablet itself held no magical properties. A solid piece of Leymetal that size is easily worth hundered platinum dragons. Whoever made it must have either possessed great wealth or believe the tablet to be of great importance.

We quickly confirmed the text was indeed celestial but were unable to decipher its meaning. This was due in part to a damaged portion of the tablet which tragically ruined some of the script. Moreover, the difficulty stemmed from the strange nature of celestial, the language of the divine. One can completely read the text and grasp the meaning of each word, yet for some unknown reason still not understand the greater message. Like the gods themselves celestial is sometimes inscrutable. This phenomenon has been observed and well documented. I myself have reviewed celestial texts from the Order's archives and on numerous occasions simply been unable to fathom what I had read. I must admit that it is a terrifying sensation. It is as if the mind itself rejects that which it is incapable (or unworthy) of receiving. Only after long and arduous hours of reflection, worship, and review of the text will the meaning reveal itself and even sometimes still it only happens under strange circumstances. I have long considered the fickle nature of the gods to be one of life's few undeniable truths.

Kiona left the work of teasing out the meaning of the text to us, claiming Haskell's precociousness and my "pedantry" would bring about results in short order. Privately, I suspected she had found something that interested her far more than the tablet: a tattered scroll made of thick gray vellum, topped with a twisted skull. I only saw it for the faintest moment but it made my blood run cold. I'm not so ignorant as to question a Maven's area of research but I've always found some of Kiona's choices... risky.

For twelve days and twelve nights Haskell and I threw ourselves into our work. We spent the first few days in meditation and prayer, opening ourselves up to Amanautor's divine wisdom. We then began subjecting the tablet to rites and rituals. By the 10th day we had begun sleeping with it, each alternating shifts. We reasoned that many prophets had received divine proclamations in their dreams... though we both were growing desperate.

It was early in the morning of the 13th day when Haskell shook me from my sleep, eyes wide and honestly crazed, though I imagine I looked much the same. After finally calming him down enough to understand what he was saying, he told me he had finally cracked it. Voice shaking, he recited what he had learned:

There once were Four upon which the world did turn.

Four to blow, drown, bury and burn.

There once were Seven for which our hearts did beat.

Only with them are we complete.

There once was One which held dominion oer all.

He alone will witness the fall.

There then was Another and lo there were Two.

Upon her wings, lies were made true.

Alasadriel, oh Alasadriel.

All men and beasts did hear your knell.

The One above all felt long forgotten fear.

For once again found he a peer.

After his retelling I sat in stunned silence before making him repeat it at least a dozen more times. Enough has been written by greater scholars than I about the many interpretations of the Diluculum Versum, so I will not waste time by recounting their conclusions here. When I asked Haskell how he managed to finally unlock the meaning, he told me he indeed had a dream but couldn't remember what he saw. When he awoke he found himself standing tiptoed on the edge of his room's balcony, one hand outstretched toward Iadath, which was pitch black as it was a new moon. I'm thankful he didn't fall, as our rooms were on the fourth floor of the palace. 

After finishing our night's rest he proudly told Kiona over breakfast. She seemed quite impressed and treated us both to a fine dinner and strong drink that evening as a celebration. I daresay Haskell and myself will probably never touch Nokistran lemon brandy again for all our years, but I imagine it was still a memorable way to commemorate his unofficial knighting.
