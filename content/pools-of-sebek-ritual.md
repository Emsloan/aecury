## History

It has long been a tradition in Wahasha to make offerings to Sebek in hopes he will spare you from misfortune.

In the ancient times monthly offerings of gems would be made in Sebek's name. Tortle priests would gather offerings from every home in the Beasthold. On the night of the new moon the high priest would load a newly constructed boat with the offerings and take it to the center of the Lake of Wahasha. High priests wrote of the unnatural stillness of the water on these nights, the deafening silence, and the seeming infinite expanse below them as the stars reflected off of the glassy water. 

Once they made it to the center of the lake the high priest would light a torch and place it in a special wooden sconce. Then they would swim back to the shoreline as quickly as they could. It is said if they were unable to return before the boat burned and sank they would be devoured by Sebek himself. This was because anyone foolish enough to dally on the water surely had forgotten how wise it was to fear the Lord of Floods.

In modern times monthly offerings are still made but usually still in sight of the shore and the boats are not burned and sunk. Furthermore, as the population of Wahasha grew it became difficult to gather offerings from every person and family. Instead many created personal offering places within their own homes. The offering places need only be pools of water. They can be natural or crafted, simple or ornate, and miniscule or massive.

The offering pools have become so ubiquitous that they now serve a dual purpose as an example of a family or individuals wealth. Incredibly wealthy homes often have entire rooms dedicated to their offering pools and it is common to have guests make token offerings as a sign of respect to their hosts. 

In following with the tradition of sinking gems to the bottom of the lake it is considered incredibly taboo to remove any gems from the pools. A gem offered is a gift to Sebek and one does not take back a gift to a god lightly. However, it is not unheard of for those fallen on hard times to retrieve some wealth from within their pools, only to return them later once their troubles have passed. Many of the younger Wahashans have even started treating the pools as mere storage places for their gems, to be filled and empty as they please.

It is said that stealing from a pool bestows a curse on the thief and this is widely accepted as true for the offerings in the lake. However, personal pools make excellent targets for thieves of weak morals and many families choose to invest in more tangible security measures. Poor families often have small pools that can be locked in boxes or closets. Wealthier families have been known to hire guards and construct complex traps and alarms. The wealthiest families strive for the appearance of no security measures whatsoever as a sign of their faithfulness. Would be thieves should be wary, as often these pools are protected by invisible but powerful magical means.
